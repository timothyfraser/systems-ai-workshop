Why do outbreaks spread faster in some networks than others?

🎯 Big Picture

Two communities can face the same germ and see very different outbreaks. The difference is often not the germ — it's the shape of the contact network: who is connected to whom. Below, you run the outbreak yourself.

🔍 Walkthrough

Each node is a person and each edge is a contact. A node's degree is how many contacts it has; the big nodes are hubs. The simulation is the classic S‑I‑R model: people start Susceptible, become Infected for one round (when they can pass it on), then Recover and stay immune.

Each round is one step outward through the network — the same idea as a degree of separation. The round counter tells you how many hops it took the germ to travel from patient zero to everyone. Seed a hub and that number is small; seed a node out on the fringe and the germ has to crawl, round by round, before it reaches the core.

Drop contagiousness below 100% and transmission becomes a coin flip on every contact — now the outbreak can fizzle out early and never consume the whole network, just like a real one that dies down before everyone is exposed.

✅ Learning Checks

Q1. You can seed the outbreak anywhere. Which choice infects the whole network in the fewest rounds — a hub or a fringe node? Try both and read the round counter.

💡 Hint

Count the hops from your seed to the farthest person.

✅ Show answer

A hub. Because it sits a few hops from everyone, the germ reaches the whole network in fewer rounds — that short average path length is exactly why hubs drive fast outbreaks.

Q2. Set contagiousness to 40% and run it a few times from the same node. Why is the final size different each time?

💡 Hint

Every contact is now a chance, not a certainty.

✅ Show answer

Transmission is now stochastic — each edge either passes the germ or doesn't. Some runs the germ finds a hub early and explodes; others it dies in a quiet corner. Same network, different luck.

Q3. What does the "Recovered" count represent, and why does the "Infected" count rise and then fall?

💡 Hint

People are only infectious for one round.

✅ Show answer

Recovered = people who already had it and are now immune. Infected is the moving wave front: it grows while there are fresh susceptibles to reach, then collapses once the germ runs out of new contacts. That rise-and-fall is the classic epidemic curve.

🔁 Recap

Outbreaks spread faster where the network has hubs and short path lengths — you just watched a hub seed consume the network in a handful of rounds while a fringe seed took many more. Structure, not just the germ, drives the curve.
