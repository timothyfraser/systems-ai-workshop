Why do outbreaks spread faster in some networks than others?

🎯 Big Picture

Two communities can face the same germ and see very different outbreaks. The difference is often not the germ — it's the shape of the contact network: who is connected to whom.

🗺️ The Map

🔍 Walkthrough

A node is a person; an edge is a contact between two people. A node's degree is how many contacts it has. A few high-degree nodes — hubs — can act as super-spreaders, reaching many others at once.

Two more shape factors matter: short average path length (everyone is only a few hops apart) lets a germ reach the whole network quickly, while high clustering (your contacts know each other) can slow it by trapping it in tight groups.

✅ Learning Checks

Q1. Why can one hub change an outbreak so much?

💡 Hint

Think about how many edges a hub has.

✅ Show answer

A hub's high degree means one infection there exposes many contacts at once — a super-spreading event.

Q2. Does shorter average path length speed up or slow down spread?

💡 Hint

Fewer hops to reach anyone.

✅ Show answer

Speeds it up — the germ needs fewer steps to reach the whole network.

Q3. How can high clustering slow an outbreak?

💡 Hint

Where do infections get "stuck"?

✅ Show answer

When your contacts also contact each other, infections recirculate inside a tight group instead of jumping to new ones.

🔁 Recap

Outbreaks spread faster where the network has hubs, short path lengths, and low clustering — structure, not just the germ, drives the curve.
