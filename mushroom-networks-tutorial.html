🔍 Walkthrough

Why Structure Matters

The network isn't random. In Ithaca, some people are "hubs"—like restaurant owners, farmers market organizers, or popular foraging guides. They connect many other people. Others are on the fringe, with few connections. When mushroom knowledge starts at a hub, it spreads quickly because those connectors reach many people at once. When it starts on the fringe, it has to hop from person to person, taking many more rounds.

The Adoption Loop

Each round, anyone actively exploring (red) has a chance to convert their neighbors. The "Contagiousness" slider controls this probability—think of it as "how persuasive is the person who discovered the mushrooms?" A low value (20%) means most neighbors stay unconvinced; a high value (80%) means word spreads aggressively. Once someone adopts (turns green), they're a carrier—they influence others, but can't be re-infected.

Peak vs. Plateau

Notice that adoption doesn't grow forever. After a while, everyone who could be reached is reached, and the curve flattens. You can reach more of the network faster by starting at a hub (watch the hub explode) or by raising contagiousness. But the final "percent reached" also depends on network structure—isolated clusters may never adopt.

Real-World Parallel

In Ithaca, this plays out as restaurants adopting foraged mushrooms, foragers teaching classes at the Cornell Plantations or farmers markets, and local herbalists sharing recipes. The connectors—a popular chef, a well-known foraging blogger—are the real transmitters. That's why you see mushroom dishes suddenly on many menus, or why certain wild varieties become trendy: structure decides speed.

✅ Learning Checks

1. Seed a hub node (big, highly connected) and a fringe node (small, few edges). Which reaches more of the network first?

Hint: Try clicking a very large node in the center, hit Play, and watch the first 5 rounds. Then Reset and click a small, isolated node at the edge and replay.

Answer: The hub spreads much faster. In the first 5 rounds, a hub can reach dozens of nodes; the fringe spreads slowly because it must jump one neighbor at a time. This is why influential people and venues (restaurants, farmers markets, popular foragers) are so important for spreading practices.

2. Raise the contagiousness to 80% and lower it to 20%. What changes?

Hint: Seed the same node twice—once with each slider setting—and count the rounds it takes to reach 50% of the network.

Answer: At high contagiousness, adoption spreads in far fewer rounds. At low contagiousness, even hubs take many rounds. In reality, this is like the difference between a truly viral mushroom (everyone wants to cook it) and a niche interest (few people care enough to mention it).

3. Is it possible to reach 100% of the network? Why or why not?

Hint: Try different starting nodes and contagiousness values and watch the final percentage.

Answer: No. This network has isolated components—small clusters of people who aren't connected to the main group. An idea can't jump the gap unless someone physically moves between clusters (or the network changes). In Ithaca, this might mean certain families or closed groups don't hear about a foraging trend.

🔁 Recap

Network structure shapes how fast practices spread. A single person at the right place (a hub) can ignite adoption across the entire network in just a few rounds, while the same idea at the fringe crawls slowly from person to person. Contagiousness matters, but so does position. In Ithaca, this is why chefs, farmers markets, and well-connected foragers drive mushroom trends—they're the hubs. And some groups may never adopt, not because they lack interest, but because they're structurally isolated.
